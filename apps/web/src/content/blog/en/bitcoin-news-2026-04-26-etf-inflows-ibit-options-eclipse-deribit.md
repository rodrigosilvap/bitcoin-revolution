## Introduction

Bitcoin's April rebound is no longer just a price story. Beneath the headline number — BTC holding above $77,000 and tracking its strongest month in a year — two structural milestones have quietly arrived that say more about the next decade of Bitcoin than any single candle on the chart.

First, U.S. spot Bitcoin ETFs have logged their longest and largest inflow streak since the October 2025 peak, pulling in roughly **$2.4 billion** in April with several trading days still left on the calendar. Second, open interest in BlackRock's IBIT options has, for the first time ever, surpassed Deribit — the offshore venue that has dominated Bitcoin derivatives for the better part of a decade.

These are not parallel headlines. They are the same story, told from two different angles: regulated, U.S.-based institutional capital is now the marginal buyer and the marginal hedger of Bitcoin.

## The ETF Streak: $2.4 Billion in Eight Days

Between April 14 and April 24, U.S. spot Bitcoin ETFs recorded eight consecutive days of net inflows, totalling **$2.09 billion** across the streak. Adding the days on either side, the month-to-date figure now sits at roughly $2.4 billion — and April still has trading days remaining.

To put that into perspective, this is the strongest run for the category since the late September to early October 2025 window, when Bitcoin was racing toward its $126,000 all-time high and ETFs absorbed roughly $5.33 billion over a similar nine-day stretch.

**The cadence of the recovery is what stands out:**

* Week ending April 10: **$786 million** in net inflows
* Week ending April 17: **$996 million**
* Week ending April 24: **$823 million**
* Single-day peak: **$223 million** on Thursday alone

Every rolling tracking period — daily, weekly, monthly — has now turned positive. After four consecutive months of net outflows that began with the October 2025 selloff, the door has flipped from "exit" to "entrance."

---

## Why This Streak Matters More Than the Price

ETF flows are the closest thing Bitcoin has to a clean institutional signal. Unlike on-chain volume, which mixes whales, exchanges, miners, and bots, spot ETF inflows almost always represent an advisor, a pension allocator, a family office, or a corporate treasurer making a deliberate, paperwork-heavy decision to add Bitcoin exposure.

That cohort doesn't trade headlines. It trades models, mandates, and quarterly reviews.

When that crowd switches from net selling to net buying for eight days running, it tells you something specific: the institutional thesis on Bitcoin survived the Q1 drawdown. The story they told their investment committees in 2024 — that Bitcoin belongs in a diversified portfolio as a non-sovereign, scarce, programmable asset — was not abandoned at the first sign of volatility.

That is exactly the kind of behaviour you want to see from "sticky" capital.

## IBIT Options Eclipse Deribit: A Tectonic Shift

The second milestone is, in many ways, even more telling. On Friday, April 25, open interest in BlackRock's IBIT options reached **$27.61 billion**, edging past Deribit's $26.90 billion in Bitcoin options open interest for the first time in history.

For context, Deribit has been the undisputed home of Bitcoin derivatives since 2016. Hedge funds, trading desks, market makers, and crypto-native treasuries have all routed serious size through Deribit's order books. The fact that a single ETF's listed options now outweigh Deribit's entire Bitcoin options book on a Friday close is a moment the industry will be referencing for years.

**Some details from beneath that headline:**

* IBIT cumulative net ETF inflows have crossed **$58.33 billion** since launch, placing the fund in the top 1% of all U.S. ETFs by lifetime flows.
* Open interest in IBIT options is concentrated around bullish strikes near **$109,700**, with weight on October 2026 expiries.
* Deribit's positioning is centred around **$106,000** strikes and August 2026 expiries — meaning IBIT's exposure is, on average, roughly two months longer-dated.
* IBIT implied volatility runs slightly above Deribit's, in part because ETF holders cannot easily short Bitcoin and must rely on put options to hedge — a structural quirk that keeps demand for protection persistently elevated.

## What "Onshore" Really Means for Bitcoin

The migration of derivatives activity from Deribit to IBIT is not a marketing story. It changes the plumbing of the Bitcoin market.

**A regulated, onshore options venue means:**

* **Pension funds and endowments** can now express Bitcoin views through instruments their compliance teams already understand.
* **Banks and broker-dealers** can build structured products — capital-protected notes, covered-call funds, defined-outcome strategies — without touching offshore counterparties.
* **Tax and reporting frameworks** are domestic, removing one of the last operational barriers to large-allocator participation.
* **Counterparty risk** sits with U.S.-regulated entities rather than with offshore exchanges that traders historically had to vet themselves.

The cumulative effect is that Bitcoin's market microstructure is being rebuilt around the same rails that already carry equities, rates, and credit. Once that infrastructure is in place, allocations rarely shrink — they compound.

## The Short-Term Holders Are Selling — And That's Fine

A more nuanced data point sits underneath the inflow story: **short-term holders are quietly distributing into the strength.** Coins that moved within the last 155 days have been flowing to exchanges as Bitcoin grinded back from $74,000 toward $78,000.

This is not a bearish signal in the way it sounds. In every healthy Bitcoin recovery, the same pattern shows up:

* Short-term holders, who bought near recent highs, sell into the bounce to break even.
* Long-term holders absorb the supply.
* New institutional buyers — in this cycle, ETFs — quietly mop up what's left.

That coin redistribution is precisely what creates the "wall of supply" that markets need to chew through before the next leg up. The fact that ETFs are absorbing $200 million-plus on individual days while short-term holders unload tells you the bid is real.

## Risks Worth Watching

This is a bullish setup, but no setup is free of risk. The honest list:

* **A failed retest of $80,000.** That level remains the key technical barrier. A clean break invites trend followers and structured-product hedgers; a rejection could reignite short-term holder selling.
* **Macro liquidity reversal.** Stablecoin growth (USDT alone has expanded by roughly $5 billion in recent weeks) is a tailwind, but a sudden hawkish pivot from the Fed could compress all risk assets simultaneously.
* **Geopolitical shocks.** The Iran ceasefire extension is a positive, but markets have learned that geopolitical calm in 2026 has a short half-life.
* **ETF inflow exhaustion.** Eight-day streaks eventually end. The question is not whether the streak breaks, but what flows look like in the week after.

These are real, but they are tactical risks against a structural backdrop that has clearly improved.

---

## Conclusion

For most of Bitcoin's existence, the question was whether institutions would ever come. That question has been definitively answered. The new question — and the one this week's data starts to address — is whether institutions will *stay*, and whether the market structure can continue to evolve to meet them where they already operate.

A $2.4 billion inflow streak says yes on the demand side. IBIT options overtaking Deribit says yes on the plumbing side. Combined, they describe a Bitcoin market that looks less and less like the cypherpunk frontier of 2013 and more and more like a permanent fixture in the global asset allocation map.

The price chart will do whatever the price chart does next week. The structural story — the one written in ETF flow data, options open interest, and regulated derivatives — is moving in one direction, and it is not slowing down.
