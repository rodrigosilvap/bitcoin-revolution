## Introduction

On the morning of May 13, 2026, Bitcoin is trading around **$82,000**, recovering from the low-$80,000s consolidation that has defined the last month. The market is calm — but the policy calendar is not. At **10:30 AM ET tomorrow, May 14**, Chairman Tim Scott's **U.S. Senate Banking Committee** will hold a formal markup of the **Digital Asset Market Clarity Act**, a **309-page** bill that, if it survives committee and clears the floor, will be the most consequential piece of U.S. crypto legislation since the spot Bitcoin ETF approvals of January 2024.

Prediction markets are paying attention. **Polymarket traders now assign a 73% probability** to the CLARITY Act being signed into law during 2026, up from just **46% at the start of the month** — a 27-point repricing in two weeks.

For a Bitcoin investor, the question is not whether to memorize the section numbers. It is to understand **why this bill matters for the structural demand picture**, what it actually changes for BTC, and what the failure mode looks like if the vote does not go as expected.

## What the CLARITY Act Actually Does

Stripping away the jargon, the CLARITY Act tries to solve a problem that has plagued U.S. digital-asset markets for roughly a decade: **nobody knows who the regulator is, and nobody knows what the rules are.** Different agencies have asserted overlapping jurisdiction, court rulings have been inconsistent, and the result has been "regulation by enforcement" — companies finding out the rules only when they are sued.

The bill addresses this head-on by **slicing the digital asset universe into three clean regulatory buckets** and assigning each one to a primary federal regulator.

* **Digital commodities** — assets sufficiently decentralized that they look more like commodities than securities. Bitcoin is the prototypical example. Oversight goes to the **CFTC**, the same agency that regulates oil, wheat, and gold futures.
* **Restricted digital assets / digital securities** — tokens that still function like investment contracts in a centrally managed project. These stay under **SEC** jurisdiction with disclosure and registration regimes adapted to the asset class.
* **Payment stablecoins** — dollar-pegged instruments designed for transactions, regulated through a federal framework with banking-style requirements, building on the stablecoin reforms already enacted in 2025.

For **Bitcoin specifically**, that classification is the single most important sentence in the entire 309 pages. By being treated as a digital commodity under CFTC oversight, BTC moves out of the regulatory grey zone that has haunted virtually every other crypto asset and into the same broad legal category as gold and oil — a status that's already implicit in how spot ETFs were approved, but has never been written into law.

## The Stablecoin Yield Compromise

The most politically heated piece of the bill — and the one that nearly stalled it twice — is the **stablecoin yield provision**.

The core tension is straightforward. Banks worry that if crypto firms can pay yield on stablecoin balances, depositors will flee bank accounts for higher-yielding tokenized dollars, threatening the deposit base that supports lending. Crypto firms argue that legitimate stablecoin-related rewards programs are essential to a competitive market.

The compromise, hammered out by Senators **Thom Tillis** and **Angela Alsobrooks** and released ahead of tomorrow's markup, does roughly this:

* **Bans yield equivalent to bank deposit interest** on payment stablecoins themselves.
* **Permits "bona fide activities"** — meaning rewards or yield generated by separate, clearly disclosed protocols and services tied to using stablecoins, rather than the stablecoin instrument itself paying interest.

It is not a clean win for either side, but it is exactly the kind of negotiated middle ground that turns a partisan bill into something a Senate Banking Committee can actually mark up. The crypto industry has publicly backed the compromise, removing one of the largest remaining objections from the right flank of the Republican caucus and the moderate Democrats whose votes the bill needs.

## Why This Matters for Bitcoin

It is fair to ask: if Bitcoin is already trading north of $80,000 with $66 billion in BlackRock's IBIT alone, why does this bill matter? The honest answer is **structural**, not tactical.

* **It locks in CFTC oversight by statute.** Today, Bitcoin's commodity status rests on a patchwork of court rulings and agency interpretations. A change of administration at the SEC could, in principle, reopen old fights. A statute is harder to undo.
* **It unlocks the remaining institutional pools.** Many large allocators — pension funds, endowments, certain insurance subaccounts — have internal rules requiring **explicit federal statutory clarity** before they can hold an asset class. ETF approval got the first wave in. Statutory clarity gets the next wave.
* **It rebuilds the U.S. as a crypto venue.** Several of the largest digital-asset firms quietly relocated key operations offshore between 2021 and 2024 because of enforcement-driven uncertainty. A statute that defines the playing field is what makes onshoring economically rational again — which over time translates into deeper U.S. liquidity and more transparent on-shore markets.
* **It de-risks the spot ETF franchise.** The spot Bitcoin ETFs are now a $130B+ asset category. A clean statutory home for BTC reduces the legal tail risk on these products and makes it easier for issuers to expand into adjacent vehicles (options, separately managed accounts, retirement-platform integrations).

None of this changes Bitcoin's protocol. None of it changes the 450-BTC-a-day issuance schedule. But it changes the **demand-side composition** in a way that compounds over years, not days.

## The May 14 Markup: What to Watch

A "markup" is the stage where committee members offer amendments to the bill text, debate them, and vote. Three things are worth watching when the Senate Banking Committee gavels in tomorrow.

* **The size of the bipartisan vote.** A narrow party-line passage is a much weaker signal than a 15-9 or 17-7 vote that shows the bill can survive on the floor. The same Tillis-Alsobrooks dynamic that produced the yield compromise is the model the bill needs to be replicated in committee.
* **Amendments to the digital-commodity definition.** Any attempted narrowing of the "sufficiently decentralized" test could affect not just Bitcoin's classification (almost certainly unchanged) but the path other assets take to commodity status, which has indirect consequences for crypto market structure overall.
* **The stablecoin yield carve-outs.** Some senators are still expected to push for stricter language. If the compromise survives in close to its current form, the bill's odds on the floor improve materially.

A committee vote on a 309-page bill rarely makes price-action news on the day. The mechanism is slower: a clean passage tomorrow likely re-prices Polymarket's 73% probability higher, which feeds into how RIAs and institutional allocators think about their **2H 2026 model weights** for BTC. That shows up in flows over the following weeks and months.

## The Risk Scenarios

A thoughtful investor plans for the bill failing, not just succeeding. Three failure modes are worth labeling.

* **Failed markup.** The bill fails to clear committee, sending the drafters back for further revisions. Bitcoin probably trades flat to modestly weaker on the news; the structural ETF bid continues regardless, but the institutional **acceleration** stalls.
* **Diluted bill.** The committee strips the strongest provisions — perhaps narrowing the digital-commodity definition or watering down the stablecoin yield compromise. This is the **most likely** outcome statistically: a passable bill that disappoints both sides slightly. Bitcoin's reaction is mostly muted.
* **Clean passage with strong bipartisan vote.** The bull case. Polymarket repricing toward 80%+. The bill moves to the Senate floor with momentum, and the timeline for enactment into law compresses from "sometime in 2026" toward "by the August recess." This is the scenario the market is partially front-running already.

Note the asymmetry. Bitcoin's price is unlikely to fall meaningfully on a failed markup, because the **structural ETF bid** described in our [May 12 analysis](/blog/en/bitcoin-news-2026-05-12-etf-institutional-demand) is independent of this bill. But a clean passage could meaningfully accelerate the **next leg** of institutional flow.

## What This Means for the Educational Investor

If you are building a long-term mental model of how Bitcoin transitions from "speculative tech asset" to "core portfolio building block," tomorrow's vote is a small but real waypoint on that path. A few takeaways.

* **Regulation, when written well, is bullish for Bitcoin.** That sentence would have sounded heretical to crypto natives five years ago. In 2026, it is just true. Bitcoin has nothing to hide — it is open-source, transparent, and decentralized. Clear rules disadvantage opaque competitors, not BTC.
* **Statutory clarity > administrative interpretation.** ETF approvals matter. SEC rule-makings matter. But a **law passed by Congress** is the most durable form of legitimacy in American finance. That is what is on the table.
* **The bill is not the catalyst — the bill is the floor.** Bitcoin does not need the CLARITY Act to be a valid long-term asset. The protocol works regardless. What the bill does is **raise the floor on the worst-case U.S. regulatory environment**, which in turn lowers the risk premium institutions apply to BTC exposure.

The most important Bitcoin story of May 13, 2026 is not in the price chart. BTC is doing roughly what it has done all month — drifting in the low-$80,000s while institutional flows quietly accumulate in the background.

The story is **15 hours away in Hart Senate Office Building Room 538**, where the U.S. Senate Banking Committee will spend its Wednesday marking up a bill that, six months from now, may be remembered as the moment the United States stopped trying to regulate digital assets through litigation and started doing it through legislation.

For Bitcoin specifically, the message is simple: **a clean statutory home as a digital commodity under the CFTC**, decoupled from every other unresolved crypto fight, makes BTC easier to allocate to, easier to custody for institutions, and structurally harder to attack with future enforcement actions.

That is what tomorrow is really about. Not a price catalyst — a **regime change** in how the world's largest capital market thinks about its hardest money.

The 73% probability tells you the market thinks it happens. The 27% reminds you it is not yet a done deal. Watch the gavel.
