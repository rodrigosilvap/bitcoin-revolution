## The Deal Itself: What's Actually Being Priced

The SpaceX listing is unique in modern IPO history for a few reasons:

* **Size.** A $75 billion raise at a $1.75 trillion valuation makes this the **largest IPO ever**, eclipsing Saudi Aramco's 2019 record of $25.6 billion.
* **Float.** SpaceX is selling roughly **4.3% of its outstanding equity**. That keeps the public float intentionally small — which tends to amplify both upside and volatility in the first weeks of trading.
* **Anchor investors.** Multiple sovereign wealth funds, including Saudi Arabia's **PIF** and the **Abu Dhabi Investment Authority**, have reportedly committed to anchor allocations. Major endowments and pension funds round out the cornerstone book.

What this means in practice: the deal is **massively oversubscribed**, and a significant share of the capital being routed into SpaceX over the next 72 hours is coming from accounts that would otherwise be deploying into other risk-on trades — including Bitcoin ETFs.

### Why Pricing Matters More Than the Headline Valuation

The **$135 price** isn't an arbitrary number. It's calibrated so that:

* Anchor investors get a built-in cushion (the implied first-day pop is in the **10–20%** range based on grey-market indications).
* The deal can absorb early profit-taking without breaking the offering price.
* SpaceX raises a round number that funds the next decade of Starship development and Starlink expansion without needing to return to the equity market.

If the stock opens meaningfully above $150 on June 12, expect a wave of allocation rebalancing across institutional portfolios — and that's where Bitcoin enters the story.

## The 18,712 BTC Question

According to disclosures filed alongside the S-1, SpaceX held **18,712 BTC** as of **March 31, 2026**. At today's price of roughly $62,500 per coin, that's **approximately $1.17 billion** sitting on the SpaceX balance sheet.

For context, that puts SpaceX in the **top 10 of all corporate Bitcoin holders globally**, sandwiched between **Tesla** (still holding **9,720 BTC**) and the largest pure-play Bitcoin treasury company, **Strategy** (formerly MicroStrategy), which holds **581,000+ BTC**.

### What the IPO Changes for SpaceX's Bitcoin

Once SpaceX is public:

* **Transparency increases.** Every quarter, SpaceX must disclose any change to its BTC holdings in 10-Q filings. The market will know whether SpaceX is accumulating, holding, or selling.
* **Mark-to-market accounting applies.** Under **ASU 2023-08**, public companies are required to mark digital assets to market each reporting period. This means SpaceX's quarterly earnings will move with Bitcoin's price — creating a new feedback loop between BTC volatility and SpaceX's stock.
* **Indirect Bitcoin exposure becomes investable.** Pension funds and 401(k) accounts that cannot hold spot Bitcoin or even spot Bitcoin ETFs can hold SpaceX. Every dollar that buys SpaceX shares is buying a sliver of a $1.17 billion BTC position alongside it.

### Could SpaceX Sell?

This is the question that's keeping Bitcoin desks up at night. Three observations:

1. **Musk has publicly stated** that neither SpaceX nor Tesla has plans to sell their existing Bitcoin holdings. That guidance was reiterated in the S-1 risk-factor disclosures.
2. **Selling would be reputationally costly.** Both SpaceX and Tesla benefit from Musk's pro-Bitcoin alignment, and a forced sale to fund operations would invite scrutiny of cash management.
3. **The cash raised by the IPO obviates any need to sell.** SpaceX is raising $75 billion. There is no operational scenario in which liquidating $1.17 billion of BTC moves the needle for a company with $75 billion in fresh capital.

The base case is therefore that SpaceX's 18,712 BTC stays put — and the public-market float effectively creates **a new, very long-duration Bitcoin holder** with high reputational cost to selling.

## The Liquidity Drain Hypothesis

The more immediate concern for short-term price action isn't SpaceX's existing BTC. It's where the **new IPO capital is coming from**.

### ETF Outflows Tell the Story

U.S. spot Bitcoin ETFs have now logged **13 consecutive sessions of net outflows** between May 15 and June 3, shedding a cumulative **$4.33 billion** — the longest outflow streak since the products launched in January 2024.

The outflow pattern coincides almost perfectly with:

* **The SpaceX roadshow** (mid-May through early June)
* **The OpenAI tender offer** (announced May 28, closing June 30)
* **Anthropic's reported pre-IPO funding round** (Series H, expected to close before the SpaceX listing)

Family offices and crossover hedge funds have a finite allocation to **"sovereign-adjacent risk"** — assets that hedge fiat debasement and tech-platform dominance. When the universe of investable opportunities expands dramatically (SpaceX, OpenAI, Anthropic all becoming partially or fully liquid in the same six-week window), capital rotates.

This is not a bearish structural argument against Bitcoin. It is a **calendar-effect argument**: for a few weeks, Bitcoin is competing with the most exciting set of pre-public tech assets to come to market in a generation, and Bitcoin is losing the marginal-dollar competition.

### When Does the Liquidity Return?

Two mechanical events should reverse the outflow:

1. **June 12 — SpaceX trading opens.** Once the IPO is allocated and trading, the "wait and see" capital that was sitting in money-market funds for IPO participation gets redeployed.
2. **Mid-July — SpaceX lock-up clarity.** The first SpaceX 10-Q filing in mid-July will confirm BTC holdings are intact and quantify any first-quarter mark-to-market gain or loss. That removes uncertainty.

History suggests the post-IPO unwind of liquidity drain happens **within two to four weeks** of the listing. That timeline aligns with multiple analysts' projections of a **mid-to-late July Bitcoin recovery window**.

## Who's Buying Bitcoin Right Now

Beneath the surface of ETF outflows, on-chain and OTC data tell a very different story about who is positioning.

### Family Offices and Sovereigns

Multiple OTC desks have reported that **family-office demand is the strongest it has been in 18 months**. The pattern: discrete blocks of **500–2,000 BTC** trading off-exchange, often with multi-week settlement, indicating long-duration buyers rather than short-term traders.

Sovereign-wealth interest has been particularly notable. The **Norway Government Pension Fund Global** disclosed an indirect Bitcoin exposure increase via MicroStrategy shares in its Q1 13F filing, and the **Singapore Investment Corporation** is rumored to have established a direct BTC custodial relationship with a major U.S. prime broker.

### What This Tells Us

The capital flow picture as of June 10, 2026:

* **Retail (via ETFs):** Net sellers — exhausted, defensive.
* **Hedge funds:** Mixed — many reduced exposure into the SpaceX deal.
* **Family offices:** Net buyers — accumulating the $60–65K range.
* **Sovereigns / endowments:** Selectively accumulating with multi-year horizon.
* **Corporate treasuries:** Quiet — most existing treasury companies are holding, not adding (Strategy is an exception that recently sold 32 BTC to fund a preferred-stock dividend).

The composition of who is buying matters. **Long-duration capital absorbing supply from short-duration capital is, structurally, a constructive setup** — even when the price is unpleasant in the moment.

## Putting It in Perspective

Step back from the day-to-day price action and the picture is consistent with previous IPO-adjacent Bitcoin corrections:

* **2021 (Coinbase IPO):** Bitcoin peaked the day Coinbase listed and corrected for two months before resuming its bull run.
* **2024 (Reddit and Astera Labs IPOs):** Bitcoin pulled back during the IPO clustering window and resumed its uptrend within six weeks.

The pattern is not predictive — every cycle has its own dynamics — but it is **suggestive**. Liquidity rotation around mega-IPOs is a temporary feature of the market structure, not a permanent change to Bitcoin's investment case.

For long-term holders, the more important questions are:

* Is the **store-of-value thesis** intact? (Yes — global fiat debasement continues.)
* Is **institutional adoption** advancing? (Yes — ETFs hold roughly **$100 billion in AUM**, and Bitcoin is being added to public-company treasuries in larger sizes than ever.)
* Is the **supply situation** structurally tightening? (Yes — post-halving issuance is roughly **450 BTC per day**, and exchange balances continue to drift lower.)

None of these structural factors are changed by SpaceX's IPO. What the IPO changes is the **short-term liquidity context** — and that context typically resolves within weeks of the listing event.
